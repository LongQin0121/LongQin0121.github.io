Time Lines

 

The Rest is History

Conceptual Beginnings

• Vannevar Bush proposes the idea of the "Memex" in his essay "As We May Think." The Memex is a hypothetical device that allows users to create and follow associative trails of links among documents.[2][3]

 

1945

The Term 'Hyperlink'

• Ted Nelson coins the term 'hyperlink' as part of his Project Xanadu, which aimed to create a network of complex, interconnected documents.[4]

 

1960s

Hypertext Systems

• Multiple hypertext systems are developed, such as the University of Maryland's "HyperTIES" and Apple's "HyperCard," but they are confined to single computer systems or local networks.

 

1980s

Birth of the World Wide Web

• Tim Berners-Lee, while working at CERN, proposes a new information management system that uses hypertext to link documents, leading to the creation of the World Wide Web.

• Tim Berners-Lee also developed HTML,with hyperlink syntax, and introduced the world's first web browser, initially named "WorldWideWeb".[5]

 

1989

Graphical Browsers and Expansion

• Marc Andreessen and the team at NCSA release Mosaic, the first widely used graphical web browser, enhancing the usability of hyperlinks. • Mosaic later evolves into Netscape Navigator, further popularizing web browsing.

 

1993

Dynamic Web

• The introduction of JavaScript allows for more interactive hyperlinks, including those that can perform actions without reloading the page.

 

2000

SEO and the Web Ecosystem

• The practice of SEO becomes vital for websites, with hyperlinks playing a key role in influencing search rankings through link-building strategies.

• Larry Page and Sergey Brin, the founders of Google, develop the PageRank algorithm, which assesses the quality and quantity of backlinks to determine page relevance.[6]
